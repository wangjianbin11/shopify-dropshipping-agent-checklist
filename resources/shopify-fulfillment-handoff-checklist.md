## Why This Checklist Matters

Many stores can find winning products before they have a dependable fulfillment process. The problems usually appear later: SKU confusion, supplier delays, unclear QC standards, packaging mistakes, missing tracking updates, return pressure, and customer support overload.

This checklist helps sellers confirm the operational details before they move more orders into a supplier, agent, warehouse, or dedicated fulfillment partner.

## 1. Product And Variant Map

Before scaling, create one shared product map for every active SKU.

Check:

- Product title used in Shopify
- Supplier product link or factory reference
- SKU, variant, color, size, material, bundle, and accessory rules
- Product photos approved for the store
- Replacement or backup supplier option
- Items that cannot be substituted without approval

Why it matters: fulfillment errors often start when the store team, supplier, and fulfillment team use different names for the same product.

## 2. Supplier Readiness

Do not judge a supplier only by unit price. Confirm whether the supplier can support stable production, communication, small-batch testing, and future order growth.

Check:

- Can the supplier provide consistent product specs?
- Can samples or small batches be checked before volume increases?
- Is there a realistic lead-time range for current order volume?
- Is there a backup supplier path if the first supplier fails?
- Are cost changes, packaging changes, or out-of-stock risks clearly communicated?

ASG note: For normal dropshipping, small-order testing can often start with selected SKUs. Custom packaging, private label, OEM, and ODM requirements depend on the product, factory, materials, and order volume.

## 3. QC Release Standard

QC should not be treated as a slogan. A useful QC process defines what to check, what proof to keep, and what happens when a product does not meet the release standard.

Check:

- What are the must-pass visual checks?
- What function or fit checks are required?
- What packaging damage or label error should block release?
- What photos or videos should be kept as proof?
- Who decides whether to release, rework, replace, or return to supplier?

ASG note: The purpose of QC is not to promise zero defects. The purpose is to move quality risk earlier, keep evidence, and create a clear exception process when something goes wrong.

## 4. Packaging And Branding Requirements

Custom packaging is not only about putting a logo on a box. It can affect MOQ, carton size, product protection, unboxing experience, labeling, and fulfillment handling.

Check:

- Neutral packaging or branded packaging?
- Thank-you card, insert, label, sticker, manual, or hang tag?
- Product protection requirement for shipping?
- Barcode, label, country-of-origin, or platform requirement?
- Sample approval needed before mass use?
- MOQ or material constraints from the factory or packaging supplier?

Avoid broad all-product MOQ promises. The safer operating rule is to separate standard fulfillment from custom packaging and private-label work.

## 5. Inventory And Replenishment Signals

Before daily orders grow, define how stock risk will be reported.

Check:

- What inventory file or system is the source of truth?
- How often is stock checked or updated?
- Which SKUs need low-stock warnings?
- Which products can continue selling during restock, and which cannot?
- What happens if a supplier shows stock but cannot ship on time?

The goal is not only to know how many units exist. The goal is to know which units are actually available for sale and fulfillment.

## 6. Shopify Order Handoff

The fulfillment team needs enough order data to process correctly without guessing.

Check:

- Shopify order number
- Customer country and shipping method
- SKU and variant details
- Quantity and bundle rules
- Packaging notes
- Gift note or insert requirement
- Address exception rules
- Tracking upload process
- Cancellation or change-order deadline

If the order handoff is unclear, even a good supplier or warehouse can make avoidable mistakes.

## 7. Route-Dependent Delivery Expectations

Do not write one delivery promise for all products, all countries, and all shipping routes.

Check:

- Target country
- Product category and logistics restrictions
- Order processing time
- Carrier or line used
- Tracking update pattern
- Peak-season or holiday risk
- Return or reship boundary

ASG can help sellers plan route-dependent fulfillment expectations, but delivery language should always be checked against the product, destination, line, and current operating conditions.

## 8. Exception Workflow

Scaling is easier when exceptions have a written path.

Check:

- Supplier delay
- QC failed
- Packaging material missing
- Address problem
- Tracking delay
- Returned parcel
- Customer complaint
- Wrong item or damaged item

For each exception, define who owns the decision, what proof is needed, and whether the next action is refund, resend, replacement, supplier claim, packaging change, or customer communication.
